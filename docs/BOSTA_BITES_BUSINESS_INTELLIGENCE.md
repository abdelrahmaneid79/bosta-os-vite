# Bosta Bites — Executive Business Intelligence

_Permanent knowledge base for BostaOS. Compiled 2026-07-16 from a full set of owner-supplied
photographs of the real stand + packaging, cross-referenced against 20 months of live POS sales
(Oct 2024 → Jun 2026) and the owner's accounting workbook (Mar–Jul 2026)._

**Purpose.** This is not developer documentation. It is the store knowledge a CTO/strategist would
carry after spending a day inside Bosta Bites. Every future recommendation should be checked against
it so advice feels like it came from someone who has worked the floor — not a generic retail AI.

**Evidence discipline.** Every claim is tagged:
- **[Observed]** — directly visible in a photo or present in the data.
- **[Likely]** — a strong inference a seasoned retailer would make, not yet proven.
- **[Hypothesis]** — a plausible idea that must be tested before it is treated as true.
- **[Unknown]** — genuinely not yet known; do not guess it.

Nothing here invents measurements, weights, costs, supplier terms, or customer behaviour.

## 1. Business Overview

- **[Observed]** Bosta Bites is a **premium nuts / dried-fruit / candy concession** operating inside a
  host hypermarket. POS daily reports print branch label **"Gardenia Mall"** and concession/supplier
  code **351**; the mall's own fascia brand on the shelf labels is **"Hyper Hub."** A round **bosta
  bites** sticker (pink or black, peanut-with-sunglasses logo) is applied over the mall label — Bosta
  Bites is the brand the owner is building on top of the mall's infrastructure.
- **[Observed]** **Everything is sold loose, by weight** (price is EGP/kg; the POS weighs each order and
  prints net kg × price). The clamshell box is a vessel, not a fixed pack.
- **[Observed]** The mall rings every sale on its POS and **settles to the owner by cheque on a ~10-day
  cycle**, deducting a **3% commission + 15,000 EGP/month rent**. The owner therefore receives **no
  direct cash from customers** — operating cash comes only from cashed settlement cheques.
- **[Observed]** Owner-side costs are **inventory purchases** (nuts, jelly, boxes) + **salary** (worker
  4,500 + accountant/bookkeeping 2,000 ≈ 6,500/mo). Rent + commission live on the settlement side.
- **[Observed]** Scale of business: ~90k–210k EGP revenue/month (seasonal), blended gross margin ~36%.
  Peak is **March (Ramadan/Eid)** — 210k in Dec 2025, 134k in Mar 2026; summer is the trough (Jun ~91k).
- **[Observed]** Sole operator/owner is non-technical; BostaOS is his single management system.

**Why this framing matters:** Bosta Bites is a *concession*, not a standalone shop. It cannot control
rent, footfall, store hours, neighbouring categories, or the checkout. Its levers are **assortment,
packaging, pricing, placement within its own footprint, and timing of purchases to the cheque cycle.**
Strategy must respect those boundaries.

## 2. Physical Layout

- **[Observed] The main table** — a large, maroon satin-draped table, product built up in **4–5 tiers**
  toward the back, ~30+ box facings visible, plus tubs/buckets of pre-mixed product on the very top row.
  This is the primary browse-and-serve surface. A staff member in a red "Hyper Hub" polo works behind it
  with the scale.
- **[Observed] The (former) illuminated wall stand** — a black, back-lit fixture with the glowing "bosta
  bites" peanut logo and **gravity-fed clear bins** (scoop-your-own) with English category tags (Ketchup
  Peanuts, Cheese Lib, Jelly Corn, Roasted Cashew, Raw Almonds, etc.). **This fixture has been REMOVED**
  (electrical hazard — it sits over the hypermarket's live ABB electrical panels). **That wall must stay
  permanently clear**; it is not usable selling space. The mall sometimes parks its own spice buckets there.
- **[Observed] Two free-standing wire gridwall towers beside the cashier** — 4 shelves each, holding
  **smaller packs + hanging poly bags**, positioned in the checkout queue. This is the impulse/upsell zone.
- **[Observed] Adjacent, NOT owned by Bosta Bites:** a refrigerated deli case (dried-fruit slices, dates)
  to one side, and the **العطار / spice ("attar") gravity-bin section**. The owner's footprint is the
  **floor space in front of and around** these — not the fridge or spice bins themselves. The mall adds
  its own product to that floor space when it sits empty, but it is the owner's to use.
- **[Observed] Surrounding categories** (from wide shots): general-hypermarket aisles — beverages/soft
  drinks, personal care (Pantene, Signal, Listerine, Head & Shoulders), confectionery. The stand sits on a
  **main run near the checkout lanes**, i.e. high-traffic terminal position.

**Traffic flow — [Likely]:** customers reach the stand on the way to or from the tills. The stand
functions as a **destination + last-minute-add** hybrid: some come specifically for nuts; many pass it
with a full basket on the way to pay. **[Unknown]** exact approach direction and dwell time — a wide "as a
customer walks up" photo and any footfall sense would confirm.

## 3. Display Philosophy

- **[Observed]** The build is **abundance-led**: dense pyramids of filled boxes, tubs stacked high,
  every product faced multiple deep. The visual message is *plenty, fresh, turned-over daily.*
- **[Observed]** Rough current zoning on the main table (from the tiered photos): **premium nuts sit
  higher/toward the back near the scale** (served on request), while **seeds, roasted legumes, crunchy
  snacks and candy occupy the front, most-reachable rows.** Candy and snack mixes are the most
  self-service-friendly and are front-and-centre.
- **[Likely]** Placing premium nuts at the back is partly operational (they're weighed/served, higher
  value, some pilferage risk) and partly unintentional (the abundance items naturally spill forward).
- **[Hypothesis]** The most profitable candy lines and the highest-traffic nuts are **not yet
  deliberately paired** — placement looks category-clustered (all candy together, all nuts together)
  rather than engineered for cross-exposure.
- **[Observed]** No shelf-edge pricing/story cards, no "gift" call-outs, no premium plinth — the premium
  nuts are merchandised the same way as 155 EGP/kg corn snacks. Presentation does not yet signal the
  price/quality gap.

## 4. Packaging Strategy

Four clamshell (PET) formats, supplier **Kryoo Pack** (kryoopack.com), **cost confirmed 2026-07-15:**

| Format | Capacity | Cost/box | Typical role [Observed/Owner-confirmed] |
|---|---|---|---|
| Small square (SH1A) | ~250 cc | **3.20 EGP** | Premium nuts at the cashier towers — cheaper/lower-ticket entry |
| 8oz medium (workhorse) | ~237 cc | **3.27 EGP** | Default for almost everything (nuts + everyday range) |
| 500 cc (large) | 500 cc | **3.27 EGP** | Loose candy & toffees (light, fills the volume) |
| 1000 cc (biggest) | 1000 cc | **6.33 EGP** | Wrapped candy/toffee assortments + light bulky snacks |

- **[Observed]** Packaging is **flexible, not fixed per product** (owner: "I sell by weight — if someone
  wants 5kg in one bag I'll do it"). The box→product map above is *habit*, not a rule.
- **[Observed / important]** **The small, 8oz and 500cc boxes all cost ~3.25 EGP** — there is **no cost
  saving** from using a smaller box. Box choice is a **presentation + shelf-ticket** decision, never a
  cost one. Only the 1000cc (~2×) is a real cost step-up → reserve it for higher-ticket gift packs.
- **[Observed]** Packaging is a **flat ~3.25 EGP tax per pack**: trivial on a premium-nut ticket
  (<3%), but **8–23% of gross profit** on a sub-40 EGP impulse candy pack.
- **[Observed]** Fill weights cluster to hold the **shelf ticket in an approachable ~30–150 EGP band** —
  expensive-per-kg items go in smaller fills, cheap bulky candy in bigger fills. This is an intuitive but
  real "price-point packaging" instinct the owner already runs.
- **[Observed]** All packaging is **clear PET** — functional, hygienic, shows the product. **No premium
  pouch, no gift box, no branded sleeve, no ribbon.** Perceived quality is "market stall clean," not
  "premium gift." The only brand cue is the round sticker.

**Perceived-quality read [Likely]:** the clear clamshell is honest and appetising but **undersells the
premium nuts**. A 143 EGP box of pistachios and a 35 EGP box of corn snacks look like the same product
class. There is no packaging tier that says "this is a gift" or "this is the premium line."

## 5. Product Presentation

Range (all [Observed] on labels; margins/velocity [Observed] from live sales):

- **Premium nuts:** Pistachio (1100/kg), Cashew (950), Almond (870), Walnut (780), Hazelnut (1234), Raw
  Almond/Cashew. *High ticket, thin margin* (Walnut ~6–19%, Almond ~16–22%, Pistachio ~20–24%, Cashew ~34%).
- **Roasted legumes / seeds:** Fava (Aswani), Kabuki peanut, Chocolate peanut, Spanish corn, Super/Syrian/
  Egyptian-white seeds, coated seeds. *Everyday staples, mid margin, high volume* (Fava & Syrian seeds move
  hundreds of kg).
- **Candy / confectionery:** **Jamy Jelly Candy** (the hero), Jelly Sour Zone, toffees (fruit/eclair),
  marshmallow, gummies, sour belts, chocolate-coated, wafer rolls, pretzels. *Lower ticket, high margin
  (40–59%).*

**Visual hierarchy [Likely]:**
- **Dominates the eye:** the big candy mounds and colourful snack mixes (orange corn cones, sour belts,
  wrapped candy) — bright, bulky, front rows.
- **First noticed:** [Hypothesis] candy and the pre-mixed tubs, because of colour and front placement.
- **Overlooked / disappears:** [Hypothesis] the premium nuts — muted browns, higher/back placement, no
  signage — despite being the highest-ticket items and the reason a "nuts" concession has authority.

**The central tension (Observed in the data, visible in the layout):**
> **Nuts pull the crowd and the tickets, but candy carries the margin.** Jamy Jelly alone is ~19% of
> revenue at 48% margin. The premium nuts are destination/credibility items running 6–34% margin. The
> store's *image* rests on nuts; its *profit* rests on candy and snacks.

## 6. Customer Psychology

Distinguishing Observed vs inferred:
- **[Observed]** Two clearly different buying modes are served: **weighed premium nuts** (deliberate,
  served at the scale) and **grab-and-go candy/snacks** (self-select, impulse, at the front and cashier).
- **[Likely]** Shopping journey: eye is caught by the colourful candy/snack mounds → basket-adders grab a
  box of candy or a snack mix → deliberate shoppers ask for nuts by weight at the scale.
- **[Hypothesis]** Emotional triggers: *abundance* (mounds signal freshness/turnover), *colour* (candy),
  *treat/reward* (kids candy, family gathering), *gifting* (nuts for guests, occasions). Premium cue is
  currently weak; value cue (big cheap boxes) is strong.
- **[Hypothesis]** Because candy buyers stand right beside premium nuts, there is **latent cross-exposure**
  not yet converted — a candy buyer is a warm prospect for a small premium-nut add.
- **[Unknown]** Actual eye path, stopping points, and whether customers who buy nuts also buy candy in the
  same visit (basket composition) — the POS rings each product line but **not per-transaction baskets**, so
  "bought-together" cannot yet be measured. (See Unknowns.)

## 7. Operational Workflow

- **[Observed]** Serve model: staff behind the table with a **scale + POS terminal**; premium/served items
  are weighed to order; front candy/snacks are pre-boxed for self-select.
- **[Observed]** Pre-packing: many boxes are **filled in advance** and stacked (the pyramids), then
  re-weighed/priced at purchase. Pre-mixed tubs sit on the top row.
- **[Likely] Friction points:** (1) premium nuts at the back means a **reach/serve step** for the highest-
  value items; (2) pre-filled boxes must be **re-weighed at the till** (double handling); (3) refills come
  from behind/below — **[Unknown]** where backstock is held and how far the walk is.
- **[Observed]** Equipment: bench scale, POS, gridwall towers, clamshell stock. No visible sealing/branding
  station beyond the sticker.
- **[Unknown]** Opening/closing routine, who counts, how stock is replenished intraday, prep time per day.

## 8. Premium Positioning

- **[Observed]** Premium is **claimed by product** (real pistachios, cashews, walnuts, imported lines) but
  **not reinforced by presentation** (same clear box, same table, no signage, no gift format, sticker-only
  branding).
- **[Likely]** The brand is mid-market-plus in feel: cleaner and better-stocked than a souk stall, but not
  yet "premium gifting destination." The removed illuminated wall was the single strongest premium cue and
  its loss **downgraded the perceived brand** — worth noting when reasoning about brand strength.
- **[Hypothesis]** The premium nuts are **under-monetised as an image/margin asset**: they carry the
  authority but neither a premium price-realisation nor a premium presentation.

## 10. Merchandising Opportunities

_All are [Hypothesis] to be tested, grounded in what was Observed._
1. **Convert the cashier towers into a deliberate high-margin impulse engine** — load them with the proven
   margin lines (Jamy Jelly, Sour Zone ~59%, Cono Crunchies ~50%, Sticks ~54%, toffees) in **small packs
   under ~50 EGP**, not just cheaper nuts. This is the single clearest lever.
2. **Engineer nut↔candy cross-exposure** — place a small premium-nut option **beside** the candy hero so a
   warm candy buyer sees a low-commitment nut add (respecting that placement is within the owner's footprint).
3. **Give premium nuts a presentation lift** — a defined premium zone / shelf-story so a 143 EGP pistachio
   box does not read like a 35 EGP snack box.
4. **Trim the long tail** — several lines (some pretzels, bonbons) sell <1% of revenue; consolidate their
   facings onto proven winners.
5. **Lead the front rows with what actually sells** (Jamy Jelly, Cashews, Choc peanuts, Fava, Syrian seeds)
   at the most reachable, eye-level positions.

## 11. Packaging Opportunities

_All [Hypothesis]._
1. **A genuine gift format** — the 1000cc (or a new premium pouch/box) built as a **mixed nut+candy gift**
   that blends a thin-margin premium nut (perceived value) with high-margin candy (real margin) to a healthy
   combined margin and a higher ticket — especially for Eid/Ramadan/occasions.
2. **A premium tier for the premium nuts** — even a sleeve/label upgrade that visually separates them.
3. **Right-size impulse packs** — because the box is a flat ~3.25 EGP, the cheapest impulse packs bleed the
   most margin to packaging; a slightly larger/rounded impulse ticket recovers a lot of that.
4. **Stop treating box size as a cost lever** — it isn't; use it purely for the shelf-ticket band.

## 12. Workflow Opportunities

_All [Hypothesis]._
1. Bring a fast-moving premium nut or two **forward** to cut the reach/serve step on high-value items.
2. Reduce **double-handling** (pre-fill + re-weigh) where a fixed-weight pre-pack could be pre-priced.
3. Define a **daily replenishment/close routine** the app can track (ties into the existing daily-close engine).

## 18. Executive Knowledge Library

_Reusable retail principles derived specifically from Bosta Bites. These are **principles**, not
recommendations — future strategist reasoning can reference them. Each carries rationale, when it
applies, when it does not, assumptions, confidence, and photo/data evidence._

### P1 — Candy buyers are warm prospects for premium nuts
- **Rationale:** the highest-margin impulse candy and the highest-image premium nuts sit metres apart; a
  customer already treating themselves is receptive to a small premium add.
- **Applies when:** candy and premium nuts share a browse zone and the nut has a low-commitment small pack.
- **Does NOT apply when:** the nut has no small/entry pack, or placement can't put them in one sightline.
- **Assumptions:** impulse candy buyers browse; a small premium pack exists.
- **Confidence:** Medium. **Evidence:** [Observed] candy front-of-table beside nuts; [Observed] Jamy Jelly
  48% margin #1; premium nuts thin-margin but high-image.

### P2 — In this store, the box is a shelf-ticket tool, never a cost saving
- **Rationale:** small/8oz/500cc all cost ~3.25 EGP; only 1000cc is ~2×.
- **Applies when:** choosing a pack size for a weighed product.
- **Does NOT apply when:** using the 1000cc gift box (real cost step-up).
- **Assumptions:** current Kryoo Pack pricing (960/980/980/1900 per 300).
- **Confidence:** High. **Evidence:** [Owner-confirmed] box costs 2026-07-15.

### P3 — Packaging cost is negligible on premium, punishing on cheap impulse
- **Rationale:** a flat ~3.25 EGP box is <3% of a premium ticket but 8–23% of gross profit on a sub-40 EGP
  candy pack.
- **Applies when:** pricing/sizing low-ticket high-margin impulse packs.
- **Does NOT apply when:** the item is premium/high-ticket.
- **Confidence:** High. **Evidence:** [Observed] box cost vs [Observed] margins/tickets.

### P4 — Nuts are the credibility engine; candy is the profit engine
- **Rationale:** premium nuts give a "nuts concession" authority and footfall but run 6–34% margin; candy/
  snacks run 40–59% and include the #1 line.
- **Applies when:** deciding space, price, and promotion priorities.
- **Does NOT apply when:** a specific nut is both high-volume AND healthy-margin (rare here — Cashew ~34%).
- **Confidence:** High. **Evidence:** [Observed] 20 months of live margins & shares.

### P5 — Premium products deserve a premium cue, or the price gap is invisible
- **Rationale:** identical clear-box merchandising makes a 143 EGP pistachio box read like a 35 EGP snack box.
- **Applies when:** the range spans a wide price/quality gap on the same fixture.
- **Does NOT apply when:** the range is uniformly priced.
- **Confidence:** Medium. **Evidence:** [Observed] uniform clamshell + sticker-only branding; removed wall.

### P6 — This is a concession: control assortment/packaging/placement/timing, not rent/footfall/checkout
- **Rationale:** the mall owns the POS, checkout, rent, hours, and traffic; the owner controls only his
  footprint and range.
- **Applies when:** framing any recommendation.
- **Does NOT apply when:** — (always applies here).
- **Confidence:** High. **Evidence:** [Observed] settlement-by-cheque, 3% + rent, Hyper Hub POS.

### P7 — Cash is cheque-cycle cash; time purchases to it
- **Rationale:** revenue only arrives as ~10-day settlement cheques net of 3% + rent; big purchases must be
  timed to cheque receipt, not to sales.
- **Applies when:** advising restocks, MOQs, quantity breaks.
- **Does NOT apply when:** a purchase is trivially small relative to cash on hand.
- **Confidence:** High. **Evidence:** [Observed] cheque cadence + deductions.

### P8 — Gifting is a latent, unbuilt category
- **Rationale:** gifting-friendly products (premium nuts, mixed candy) + gifting occasions exist, but there
  is no gift format, so gifting spend leaks.
- **Applies when:** an occasion (Eid/Ramadan) is near AND a gift format can be offered.
- **Does NOT apply when:** no gift SKU exists yet (build first) or off-season.
- **Confidence:** Medium (Hypothesis-heavy). **Evidence:** [Observed] no gift packaging; gifting product set.

### P9 — Abundance is the store's core visual equity — protect it
- **Rationale:** the dense, fresh-looking mounds signal turnover and quality; thinning them to "tidy" would
  erode the strongest current cue.
- **Applies when:** advising facing/space changes.
- **Does NOT apply when:** abundance is on genuinely dead stock (then it signals staleness, not freshness).
- **Confidence:** Medium. **Evidence:** [Observed] the pyramids across every table photo.

## 20. Final Report

**1. Biggest things learned.**
- Bosta Bites is a **mall concession** (Gardenia Mall / Hyper Hub, code 351), sold **loose by weight**,
  settled by **~10-day cheque** net of 3% + 15,000 rent — cash is cheque-cycle cash.
- **Nuts carry the image; candy carries the profit.** Jamy Jelly is #1 revenue AND 48% margin; premium nuts
  run 6–34%.
- Packaging is a **flat ~3.25 EGP tax** (1000cc ~2×); box size is a shelf-ticket tool, not a cost lever.
- The **illuminated premium wall is gone for good** (electrical panels) — a real brand downgrade to design around.

**2. What surprised me.**
- Every box (except the biggest) costs the same — the "small box for premium" instinct saves nothing.
- The **cashier impulse towers hold cheaper nuts, not the high-margin candy** — the opposite of the profit logic.
- **June's "loss" was an accounting artifact** — a heavy stock-buying month at a real 41.6% margin, not a loss.
- The store's whole premium claim rests on ~6–34% margin products with **no premium presentation**.

**3. Strategist assumptions now known to be incorrect.**
- Any assumption of **direct customer cash** (it's cheque-only).
- Any assumption that **facings/zones/shelf-levels are known** (they're unpopulated — advice on them is
  currently ungrounded).
- Any assumption that **"bought-together" is measurable** (no basket data).
- Any assumption that a **feature/gravity wall exists** to merchandise on (removed, permanently).
- Any assumption that **purchases = cost** in-month (COGS is WAC on what sold; June proved the difference).

**4. Recommendations that become significantly better.**
- Impulse-zone (cashier tower) reload with high-margin candy — now specific and evidence-backed.
- Premium-nut presentation lift and good–better entry pack — grounded in the uniform-clamshell reality.
- Gift-format creation for occasions — grounded in the gap (no gift SKU) + gifting product set.
- Cheque-cycle-timed purchasing and MOQ/cash advice — grounded in the real settlement mechanics.

**5. Future recommendations now possible.**
